📖 Slide 4 · Reading

A tiny jungle on a sloth

Read the text. Write down three things that surprise you.

Single sleeping sloth

1. A sloth looks like a quiet animal, but its fur is full of life. In the rainforest, some sloths carry tiny moths, fungi and green algae in their fur. The fur has small cracks and grooves, so it can hold water. This makes it a good place for algae to grow.

2. The moths do not just sit on the sloth. Some sloth moths live almost their whole adult life in sloth fur. When a three-toed sloth climbs down from the tree about once a week to poop, female moths can lay eggs in the fresh dung. The larvae grow there. Later, adult moths fly back up and find a sloth.

3. Scientists think the moths may help the algae. More moths can mean more nitrogen in the fur, and nitrogen can help algae grow. The algae may help the sloth in two ways: it can make the fur look green, which is useful camouflage, and the sloth may lick and eat some algae as an extra, fatty snack.

4. Is this a perfect friendship? Not exactly. It is a complex three-way relationship. The moth gets a home and a place for its babies. The algae gets a wet place and nutrients. The sloth may get camouflage and extra nutrients. That is why many scientists describe this as a form of mutualism — but good scientists also ask: What is the evidence?

Transcript / support

A female sloth moth lives in the fur of a three-toed sloth. About once a week, the sloth climbs down from the canopy to poop at the base of a tree. This journey is risky for the sloth, because predators may be nearby. The moth leaves the fur and lays eggs in the fresh dung. The young larvae feed and grow there. When they become adult moths, they fly back into the trees and look for a sloth. In the fur, moths may add nutrients that help algae grow.
